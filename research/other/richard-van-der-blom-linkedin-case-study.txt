The Business Case
Your questions are where it starts.
The research has always been built with the audience, not just for it. Every DM I receive about why a post underperformed. Every comment where someone pushes back on conventional LinkedIn wisdom. Every question from a community member or a session attendee, all of it goes into a running library that directly shapes what we research each year.

This has been true from day one. The first report existed because two marketing teams had questions we couldn't answer from experience alone. Every edition since has started in the same place: what are practitioners genuinely struggling with, and what does the data say? The report you'll read Wednesday is not a list of things I found interesting. It's the accumulated result of thousands of questions from people doing this work every day. If you've ever sent me something in a DM or dropped a question in the comments there's a real chance it shaped a chapter.

Two data streams. One standard.
The research sits on two separate foundations that we cross-reference before publishing anything.

The first is our own dataset — approximately 100,000 posts from community members and clients, analysed manually and with selected tools and AI. This is the data we know most intimately. We can see the accounts behind the numbers, the industries, the contexts.

The second is the contribution from our research partner AuthoredUp , by far the larger dataset that brings the volume and statistical weight the research requires. AuthoredUp provides both the raw data and their own analysis and conclusions.

The rule is simple: where our findings and AuthoredUp's align, we publish. Where they partially align, we go back into the data. Where they diverge, we research until we understand why. Nothing goes in on the basis of one stream alone. The methodology section in the report covers this in more detail for anyone who wants to understand exactly how the data is collected, cleaned, and interpreted.

The writing and design process.
3,000+ hours. Five professionals involved. I started writing at the end of February, and the last two weeks have been four to six hours of writing every single day. And this alongside thinking about which insights were worth sharing in visuals and what is the best way to do that.

The writing is mine. Every chapter, every conclusion, every framing decision. I write from the data and for practitioners, people making decisions on Monday morning. The report contains complete strategies, tactics, and best practices to evaluate your own LinkedIn presence. Analytics are the foundation, but they're not the product.

The design is Nurjesa Shehadeh's, my content specialist for over 3 years now and the creative brain behind the report. Her job is to make dense research readable, to turn complex data into something a reader can absorb quickly and act on. Clarity of presentation is part of the research itself, and her contribution to this report reflects that.

The Mindset Shift
Why this costs money — and who helps make it possible.
For the first 5 editions, the report was free. When it was a quiet summer project, that made sense. It stopped making sense when the research became a year-round operation with real costs; data infrastructure, tools, design, and the sustained work of five people across twelve months.

Making it a paid product was an honest decision, not a reluctant one. The resources required to produce something at this level have only grown. Sponsors play an important role in keeping the report accessible, and I'm grateful for their continued belief in independent research.
