15 Business Strategy Problems
Introduction

I've worked with over 100 B2B businesses and I've identified problematic patterns.

Here are the 15 business strategy problems that you should avoid.

1. Lack of Focus (Shiny Object Syndrome)

The worst scenario is when you have multiple directions at the same time.

It leads to an intense lack of clarity.

Main Business
Side Business
New Idea
2. Building Tactics Before Foundations

Don't jump from tactics to tactics without a clear marketing strategy.

This leads to poor results and decision paralysis.

Examples:

Emailing
Content
ABM
Paid Ads
Podcast
PR

Foundations are unstable.

3. Founders Move in Different Directions

Founders are not aligned on the vision.

They don't do frequent "clarity checks."

This leads to an unclear roadmap.

Founder 1
Founder 2
4. The Market Demand is Not Intense Enough

A high market demand is the first key factor of business growth.

Want to sell food?

Look for a hungry crowd.

5. Pushing a Solution (With No Problem)

Starting with a product idea and then trying to find its market is bad.

This often leads to solutions looking for problems rather than the other way around.

Process:

Build a solution
Look for a problem
Spend months trying to fit the solution
6. Blaming Surface-Level Problems

Don't focus on surface-level problems.

It's an easy escape.

The real reasons are often deeper in the foundations of the business.

Example:

"Let's fire the agency!"

Surface problem:

Agency doesn't bring leads

Possible root causes:

Positioning
Changing messaging
Ego or lack of clarity
Unclear offer
Bad culture
No clear ICP
Useless product
7. Not Targeting a Specific Audience

Founders don't want to miss any business opportunity, so they target almost everyone.

Consequences:

Don't know who they are
No clear knowledge of the problem
Don't know why they buy
Can't find how they buy
8. Trying With a Broken Product

Pushing hard on marketing while the product isn't good will build business debt.

Sometimes a pivot is needed.

If your product is bad, your strategy can't be good.

9. Using a Commodity Offer

A solution similar to every other solution in the market won't be easy to market.

No differentiation leads to lower conversions.

10. The Messaging Is Messy and Unclear

Either the messaging is:

Too surface-level
Too broad
Too complex
Always changing

In every case, it's hard to understand what the business does.

Buyer reaction:

"What exactly do they do?"

11. Always Changing the Messaging

If you change your messages all the time, your audience won't be able to position you in their mind.

Typical reaction:

"I don't get what they do, it's always changing."

Example evolution:

Month 1:

We get you leads

Month 2:

We improve your sales system

Month 3:

We boost your revenue

Month 4:

We increase your ROI
12. Not Enough Efforts and Actions

The team is working at 40% while competitors are putting in 150% effort.

Momentum comes from intense effort.

Growth is directly tied to the effort provided.

13. There Is No Clear Entry Point for Buyers

The product is overall good, but it's too dense.

There isn't a strong use case that makes people think:

"Oh yes, I need this."

Too many entry points create confusion:

Entry 1
Entry 2
Entry 3
Entry 4
Entry 5

Buyer becomes overwhelmed.

14. Revenue Operations Are Not Organized

The yearly objective is not broken down into a concrete plan with targets.

As a result, actions are not enough to meet the objective.

Example:

"We are targeting $2M this year."

But there is:

No North Star Metric
No Targets Per Channel
No OKRs Per Quarter
No Weekly Actions
No Daily Actions
15. The Business Is Only Focused on the Product

The business is not solving a real painful market problem.

Instead, it is pouring money into a complex product that nobody needs.

Focus becomes:

Product Investment

Instead of:

Market Need
Customer Problem
Marketing Investment
