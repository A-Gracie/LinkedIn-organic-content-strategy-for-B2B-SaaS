**Content:** CMO: "How can we organize our marketing in 2026?"

Me: "Start from step 1 of the 95/5 Playbook:"

Step 1 - Create 2 types of content for passive buyers

~95% of your target market is not in "buying mode". But most of them are always in "learning mode": they want to keep up to date with their industry and job requirements.

Type 1: Zero-click content makes your brand discoverable 

- Thought-leadership (from the entire team is best)
- Podcasts and long form video content

Type 2: Gated content delivers deeper expertise

- Group webinars and private communities
- Industry reports or other long forms

99% of your content production volume is zero-click content as you need a constant flow for discovery. But the 1% gated content needs to be perfect.

Step 2 - Build a dynamic demand capture system

~5% are in "research or buying mode" but it doesn't necessarily mean they'll become inbound leads. They might prefer competitors or forget to contact you.

That's why you need those 2 tactics:

Tactic 1: Conversion content to turn audience into leads

- Case studies or white papers
- Solution demos to show the value proposition

Tactic 2: Warm Outbound to start conversations

- Signal-led emails and ABM
- Daily social selling 

All your efforts need to lead to:

- An optimized website (true for SLG or PLG)
- An active sales process (not a passive order taker)
- A high-converting front end offer (build this one first)

***

Run this playbook for 6 months with proper iteration and you'll have a solid marketing system in place.

**Content:** I've seen B2B Marketing change a lot in 9 years.

The biggest change is this "buying decision driver":

External voices have a growing influence on buying committees.

Each member of the committee has their own source of insights.

- "I heard in this podcast that we should do this"
- "I follow this expert, they say we should not do it"
- "I participated in a webinar saying this tool is important" 

They are influenced by external voices.

Podcasts, communities, thought leaders, online trends...

And they trust those sources more than sales reps.

Now what does it mean for your marketing strategy?

The answer is simple:

You need to be one of those "external voices".

- You need to be active in the right communities
- You can have 5 team members publish expert content
- You should host the podcast they listen to (or get invited)

Understand where your audience gets insights from, and create content there.

In a nutshell, go from only being "the seller" to "the voice worth following."

Here are the 3 biggest tactical shifts:

1. Content was a side quest for the marketing team. It's now a crucial part of their day-to-day.

2. Businesses were spending a lot to build an attribution system. Now they're investing into intent signal systems.

3. Cold email was generating pipeline but it became harder. Now signal-based outbound is the best practice.

They reflect a deep shift in B2B marketing.

And if I had to summarize, I would say:

Before: lead generation + attribution 

- "We generated 100 MQLs" 
- "Where do they come from?"

Now: demand generation + intent signals

- "We're creating demand with content"
- "What account is showing intent?"

The new playbook is about consistently creating demand and actively prioritizing who to contact. This became possible thanks to new tech (AI, tools) and new B2B buyer behavior (self-serve buying journeys, social media as a buying channel).

And of course, the foundations remain the same:

- You need a strong strategy (ICP, positioning, messaging)
- You need marketing discipline (test, analyze, iterate)
- You need data efficiency (CRM, analytics, scoring)

So if you want to install the 2026 B2B marketing playbook:

- Invest in content, partnerships, personal brand collabs
- Gather intent signals, and do warm outbound
- Build a demand capture system

## Post 6
**Date:** May 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/pierre-herubel-540b3949_this-question-will-shape-your-b2b-strategy-activity-7462124895775301632-ajXx?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFJkeLEBrvXP2UgDDYzykAJzS1BbJ8VM1rI
**Content:** This question will shape your B2B strategy in 2026:

(It impacts your offer, hires, and budget)

"Do you want to build a marketing-led or a sales-led system?"

Here's the difference:

1/Marketing-led 

The efforts are mainly on marketing actions (content, ads, emails...). So you need an offer that is easier to sell and requires fewer 1:1 conversations. 

You maximize the volume of interactions with your ICP and create a frictionless first sale. It's ideal if you target SMEs, midmarket and sell an MRR offer.

2/Sales-led

The efforts are mainly on sales actions (BDR, proposals, calls...). You spend a lot of time in 1:1 conversations but have a high-ticket offer that makes this profitable. 

You maximize the $ACV and $LTV of each deal but spend a lot of time in actions that "don't scale". It's ideal if you target upmarket and enterprises (or if you're in a vertical niche).

So the answer to this question is crucial.

It impacts everything else:

- Your offer: do you want a frictionless MRR offer or a high-ticket offer that requires long sales cycles?

- Your ICP: do you prefer to target midmarket or enterprises? (to be exact, your ICP affects the decision and you can later expand to another ICP with a secondary motion)

- Your day-to-day: do you want to spend your time optimizing 1:1 sales conversations or 1:many marketing actions?

- It also impacts your hires (sales or marketers?), your budget, your energy, your agenda...

I chose a marketing-led approach for my business.

It fits my personality and my skills as a founder.

We still do sales calls, but the heavy lifting is done by content and marketing.

**Content:** I've helped over 100 brands fix their content strategy.

And most of them face the SAME problem:

They focus on the top layers of content before building the foundations.

- They obsess over design, hooks, and storytelling...
- but skip the structure that makes content work long-term.

It’s like decorating a tasteless cake.

The audience might try, but will not get back to the content.

And that core problem splits into 4 sub-problems:

- Sub-Problem (1): They spend hours tweaking visuals instead of clarifying their ICP.

- Sub-Problem (2): They post daily but don’t follow consistent content pillars.

- Sub-Problem (3): They copy formats and ideas from others but don’t have a content plan.

- Sub-Problem (4): They start from scratch every day, without a repurposing strategy.

They’re trying to “fix” aesthetics while the real problem is deeper; their content creation lacks structure.

If you want to build a real content creation system, work on these 7 steps:

1. Define Your ICP: Know exactly who you’re talking to.
2. Messaging: Clarify what you solve and why you’re different.
3. Content Pillars: Focus on 3-5 topics that show your expertise.
4. Content Plan: Organize ideas and cadence for consistency.
5. Repurposing: Plan to turn one idea into many formats.
6. Copywriting: Write clearly about a specific topic.
7. Design: Make visuals support the message.

8. Bonus step: create your signature formats.

Which step do you find the most difficult?
